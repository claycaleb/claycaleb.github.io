Summer Reading List

JUN 8, 2024

I love books. Each year in June I get to take a mini sabbatical and I’ve decided to spend my free time reading and writing. To use Cal Newport’s language, reading and writing is how I try to cultivate a life of depth. Reading takes time. It’s slow and hearty, like a good home-cooked meal. The best books take years to write and edit and they don’t fight for your attention like online media does. There is an unlimited amount of processed content out there - news, short-form videos like TikTok or Instagram Reels, reality TV, social media - and I do everything I can to avoid that kind of content. 

While I do think more people should carefully consider what they consume, I don’t want to make it seem like I’m somehow morally superior. I think of content like food and exercise. If you want better outputs, improve your inputs. If you want Jake Gyllenhaal’s Road House body, you’ll need to eat and train like a UFC fighter. I want to be a better writer, so I choose to read books. I also want my kids to enjoy reading and I want to model that for them.

Here is my summer reading list:

  • Clear Thinking by Shane Parrish

  • These 6 Things by Dave Stuart Jr.

  • The Will to Learn by Dave Stuart Jr.

  • The Road to Wigan Pier by George Orwell

  • Brave New World by Aldous Huxley

  • You Can Make This Place Beautiful by Maggie Smith

  • The Anxious Generation by Jon Haidt

  • Range by David Epstein

  • Focus by Mike Schmoker

  • Crazy Wisdom by Chogyam Trungpa Rinpoche (re-read)

Books I’ve read so far this year:

  • The 80/20 Principle by Richard Koch

  • The Dip by Seth Godin

  • Deep Work by Cal Newport

  • Hidden Potential by Adam Grant (audiobook*)

  • Cutting Through Spiritual Materialism by Chogyam Trungpa Rinpoche

  • Feel Good Productivity by Ali Abdaal (audiobook)

  • Get Better at Anything by Scott H. Young (audiobook*)

* I finished like half of it and realized it'd be better to buy the physical copy.

Moving forward I’ll likely only listen to fiction audiobooks. Listening to nonfiction feels productive and I absorb general concepts and ideas, but I retain more when I can hold a book, write in it, and review it. I'd like to read more fiction - I’ll probably revise my list.
